## Introduction

When a potential customer searches "restaurant near me" or "lawyer in Lisbon", Google doesn't show the most beautiful website — it shows the businesses it considers most relevant, nearby, and trustworthy. That set of results is called the **Local Pack**, and appearing in it can be the difference between your phone ringing or staying silent.

In 2026, Google Maps has become even more competitive. The integration of generative AI (AI Overviews) has changed how local results are presented. But the good news for SMEs is that the fundamentals remain the same — and most of your competitors are still ignoring them.

This guide shows you exactly what to do.

---

## What is Google Business Profile (and why it's your most important asset)

**Google Business Profile (GBP)** — previously called Google My Business — is the dashboard that controls how your business appears on Google Maps and in local searches. It's free. It's powerful. And it's surprisingly underused.

When properly set up, GBP allows your business to appear:
- In the **Local Pack** (the 3 results with a map at the top of the search)
- In the information sidebar (*Knowledge Panel*) when people search directly for your business name
- On Google Maps when someone explores a geographic area

Without an optimised profile, you're invisible to local searchers — even if you have an excellent website.

---

## Step 1 — Claim and verify your profile

If you don't yet have a profile created or verified, this is the mandatory first step.

1. Go to [business.google.com](https://business.google.com)
2. Search for your business name
3. If a profile already exists (automatically created by Google), **claim it**
4. If it doesn't exist, create one from scratch
5. Complete the **verification** process — usually by code sent by post, video, or phone call

> ⚠️ **Warning:** Never skip verification. An unverified profile has limited visibility and you can't respond to reviews.

---

## Step 2 — Fill in your profile 100%

Google uses a concept called **"profile completeness"** as a ranking factor. The more valid information you provide, the higher the likelihood of being shown.

| Field | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Business name | Must be the real name — no artificial keywords |
| Primary category | The strongest signal to Google about what you do |
| Secondary categories | Allow you to appear in adjacent searches |
| Full address | Essential for geolocation |
| Opening hours | Update on holidays — Google penalises inconsistencies |
| Phone number | Local format |
| Website | Point to the most relevant page, not necessarily the homepage |
| Business description | 750 characters — use your main keyword naturally |
| Attributes | "Wheelchair accessible", "Free Wi-Fi" — overlooked by most |

## Step 3 — Photos: more important than you think

Google measures engagement on your profile. Profiles with quality photos receive significantly more website clicks and direction requests.

What you should publish:
- **Cover photo:** exterior of the premises, recognisable and current
- **Profile photo/logo:** clean background, legible as a thumbnail
- **Interior:** real atmosphere, not stock photos
- **Team:** humanises the business
- **Products/services:** especially for restaurants, clinics, shops

Recommended frequency: at least **1 new photo per month**.

> 💡 **Tip:** Name your files before uploading. `lisbon-restaurant-interior-2026.jpg` is better than `IMG_4823.jpg` — Google reads the metadata.

---

## Step 4 — Reviews: the factor with the most impact

Reviews are probably the most powerful local ranking signal that exists. But there are nuances that most SMEs don't know.

**Quantity vs. quality:** A business with 50 reviews at 4.6 stars tends to outrank one with 10 reviews at 5.0 stars. Google is suspicious of profiles with few perfect reviews.

**Accumulation speed:** Getting 30 reviews in 2 days looks suspicious. Organic, gradual growth is preferable.

**Owner responses:** Responding to reviews — positive and negative — is a signal of activity and credibility. Never ignore a negative review. A professional and empathetic response does more for your reputation than the review itself.

**How to ask for reviews (without violating Google's rules):**
- At the end of a service/purchase, send a message with the direct link to review
- Place a visible QR code in the physical space
- Add the link to your email signature

> ⛔ **What you should never do:** buy reviews, ask employees to review, or offer incentives in exchange for reviews. Google detects suspicious patterns and may suspend the profile.

---

## Step 5 — Google Posts: the feature nobody uses

**Google Posts** are publications that appear directly on your Google Maps profile. They're free, have immediate visibility, and the vast majority of businesses never use them.

Available types:
- **Update:** general news, announcements
- **Offer:** promotions with start and end dates
- **Event:** with date, time and description
- **Product:** with photo, price and description

Recommended frequency: 1 post per week. Each post lasts 7 days (except events and offers).

---

## Step 6 — NAP Consistency

This technical concept has a direct impact on ranking. Google cross-references your GBP information with other online sources (directories, website, social media) to verify the consistency of NAP — **N**ame, **A**ddress, **P**hone.

If your business appears with different names on each platform, you're creating contradictory signals for Google.

**Quick audit:**
1. Search your business name on Google
2. Check all the places it appears (directories, social media, website)
3. Ensure that Name, Address and Phone are **exactly the same** everywhere

---

## Step 7 — Monitor your results

GBP has a native analytics panel that shows:
- How many times your profile was viewed
- How many searches resulted in website clicks
- How many direction requests were made
- How many calls were generated

Look at these numbers monthly. If views are stable but website clicks are low, the problem may be your photos or description.

---

## What changed in 2026: AI and local search

With the expansion of Google's **AI Overviews**, some local searches now have AI-generated responses before traditional results. To appear in those responses:

- Your website needs to have structured, relevant content about your area of activity
- The GBP needs to be complete and up to date
- Reviews need to mention relevant terms — Google uses review text as a semantic signal

---

## Conclusion

Appearing on Google Maps isn't magic — it's consistency. A well-built profile, updated photos, genuine reviews and regular posts make the difference between being on the first page or being ignored.
